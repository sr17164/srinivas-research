## Executive Summary

<ResearchPanel label="Current view" title="The risk premium is real, but the medium-term balance is weakening">

I am bearish Brent crude over the next three to six months. Renewed US-Iran hostilities can still generate violent price spikes, yet the central case is that Gulf production and tanker flows continue to recover while the crude balance moves back towards surplus later in 2026.

The key distinction is between a **tight product market** and a **durably tight crude market**. Refinery outages and low product availability can keep gasoline and distillate cracks elevated even while crude supply recovers. That combination can support prompt volatility without sustaining Brent near conflict-premium levels.

My base case is **$70–80/bbl in 4Q26**, centred around the EIA's $70/bbl quarterly forecast but allowing for a persistent geopolitical premium. The view is invalidated by a renewed, sustained loss of Gulf export capacity or by physical balances tightening despite improving flows.

## Investment Thesis

### 1. The Market Is Pricing an Ongoing Disruption, Not a Permanent Loss of Supply

Oil prices have repeatedly repriced the probability of renewed Gulf disruption. That risk is legitimate: the Strait of Hormuz remains the world's most important oil transit chokepoint, and shipping, insurance and infrastructure constraints can create nonlinear upside.

However, the physical system has already shown an ability to adapt. The IEA estimated that Gulf oil exports rose by **6.5 mb/d in June to 16.1 mb/d**, while global supply rebounded by **4.1 mb/d**. Output remained below pre-conflict levels, but the direction of travel changed materially.

More recent shipping data reinforced both sides of the argument. Reuters reported on 19 July that exports from major Gulf producers had risen by approximately 16% from June to around 12–13 mb/d, while remaining about 32% below the pre-war February peak and slowing again as hostilities resumed.

At elevated prices, Brent therefore needs repeated evidence of lost barrels merely to hold its risk premium. A continued recovery in flows can pressure prices even without a comprehensive political settlement, but renewed transit disruption remains the central invalidation risk.

### 2. The Fourth-Quarter Balance Is the Core Bearish Catalyst

The EIA expects most regional production and trade patterns to return close to pre-conflict levels by year-end. Its July outlook forecasts global inventories moving from a **2.2 mb/d draw in 3Q26** to a **2.7 mb/d build in 4Q26** as supply recovers faster than consumption.

The IEA reaches a directionally similar conclusion. Its July report states that the global balance is set to swing back towards surplus later in the year, conditional on a gradual recovery in tanker flows.

That timing matters. The bearish thesis does not require immediate oversupply in the prompt market. It requires the market to discount a looser fourth quarter before the physical builds are fully visible.

### 3. Full-Year Demand Is Weak Even as Consumption Recovers From the May Trough

The IEA expects global demand to decline by **1.0 mb/d in 2026**, while the EIA estimates a **1.2 mb/d decline**. Both forecasts assume a recovery from the extreme disruption-driven trough, but neither implies strong full-year consumption.

This is not a conventional recessionary oil thesis. Some of the demand weakness reflects high prices, shortages and government conservation measures during the conflict. Nevertheless, it demonstrates that end users can reduce or defer consumption when the delivered cost of energy becomes sufficiently high.

Demand recovery in 4Q26 may therefore coexist with a looser balance if supply normalises faster.

### 4. Tight Product Markets Do Not Necessarily Mean Tight Crude

Global refinery runs remained approximately **6 mb/d below the prior year in June**, and the IEA expects runs to fall by **2.4 mb/d in 2026**. Gulf export refineries had not fully restarted, Russian throughput was constrained and parts of Asia continued to operate at reduced rates.

This creates an unusual split: crude availability can improve while product markets remain tight. Strong cracks and refinery margins are therefore not sufficient evidence that Brent itself should retain the full geopolitical premium.

For the crude balance, lower refinery throughput reduces immediate feedstock demand and can accelerate inventory rebuilding once tanker flows recover.

### 5. Official Forecasts Provide a Useful Directional Anchor

The EIA forecasts Brent falling from an average of **$103/bbl in 2Q26 to $70/bbl in 4Q26**, then averaging $65/bbl in 2027. This is not a precise target and should not be treated as one. It is a balance-based anchor showing how much downside is possible if production and trade recover broadly as expected.

My $70–80/bbl base-case range is deliberately wider and more conservative. It assumes that a residual geopolitical premium persists and that inventory rebuilding is slower or less visible than the EIA's central case.

### 6. The View Is Bearish, Not Complacent

The largest risk is discontinuous rather than incremental. A renewed closure of Hormuz, direct damage to key export terminals or coordinated production restraint could rapidly invalidate the thesis.

The correct implementation is therefore asymmetric: avoid uncapped short exposure, use defined risk and prefer rallies that improve the entry price. The thesis is about expected normalisation over months, not the absence of tail risk over days.

## Principal Risks

<ResearchPanel label="Downside analysis" title="What could invalidate the bearish view">

- Persistent losses from major Gulf producers or export terminals
- Coordinated OPEC+ cuts that offset recovering supply
- Faster-than-expected demand recovery
- Low onshore inventories amplifying new disruptions
- Escalation that simultaneously affects crude production and refining capacity

## Conclusion

Brent remains exposed to extreme geopolitical outcomes, but the medium-term base case is becoming less supportive. Supply has started to recover, full-year demand remains weak, refinery throughput is constrained and both the EIA and IEA expect the balance to loosen later in 2026 if Gulf flows continue to normalise.

The appropriate stance is bearish over three to six months, with a **$70–80/bbl 4Q26 base-case range**. The thesis should be expressed with disciplined risk limits because its principal failure mode is a sudden physical disruption, not a gradual forecasting error.

## Research Methodology

This note triangulates official agency balances, benchmark-price context, refinery data and physical-flow indicators. The scenario ranges are judgemental rather than outputs from a proprietary pricing model. The analysis separates prompt geopolitical risk, product-market tightness and the medium-term crude balance rather than treating them as a single signal.
