## Executive Summary

<ResearchPanel label="Executive summary" title="A sound balance-sheet thesis with a failed price call">

On 6 November 2025, I recommended shorting January 2026 ICE Brent futures at approximately $63–64 per barrel. The thesis was that non-OPEC supply growth, the gradual reversal of OPEC+ cuts, softer import demand and rising inventories would push Brent toward $58–60 before the contract expired.

The balance argument was reasonable and was subsequently echoed by the US Energy Information Administration, which forecast in November that Brent would average $55 in 2026. The trade itself did not work. Brent spot briefly traded close to $60 in December but did not reach the stated $58–60 target on the weekly series, and it rose sharply through January to more than $70 by month-end.

The failure was primarily one of timing and risk construction. The thesis required a slow physical surplus to dominate a short-dated contract whose price remained highly sensitive to geopolitical and positioning shocks.

</ResearchPanel>

## Market Background

Brent had already fallen materially from its earlier highs. That created an important challenge: the trade was not shorting an obviously expensive market. It was betting that a developing surplus would outweigh the amount of bearish information already embedded near $63–64.

The original research highlighted four sources of pressure:

- Continued growth from the United States, Brazil, Canada and Guyana
- OPEC+ restoring previously curtailed barrels
- Chinese imports being absorbed partly into storage rather than stronger refinery runs
- A mild move toward contango and rising inventories signalling weaker immediate scarcity

The variant perception was that the market still carried too much geopolitical and OPEC-discipline premium relative to the emerging physical balance.

---

## Investment Thesis

### 1. Non-OPEC Supply Was Expanding

The central bearish force was supply outside OPEC. US output remained near record levels, while newer projects in Brazil, Canada and Guyana increased the volume of barrels competing for refinery demand.

A supply thesis becomes most powerful when production growth is relatively insensitive to a modest price decline. Projects already sanctioned and operating can continue producing even when the forward outlook weakens.

### 2. OPEC+ Was Gradually Returning Barrels

OPEC+ had begun reversing some voluntary cuts during 2025. The original presentation cited intended increases of roughly 137,000 barrels per day for November and December.

The risk was that the group could pause or reverse those additions if prices weakened. This made the thesis endogenous: the more successful the short became, the greater the incentive for producers to respond.

### 3. Demand Growth Looked Fragile

China's transition toward electric transport and weaker manufacturing growth reduced confidence in the historic relationship between economic expansion and oil-import growth. Europe also remained a relatively mature demand market.

However, "declining global demand" was too strong a description. The more accurate thesis was that **demand growth was insufficient to absorb supply growth at prevailing prices**.

### 4. Inventories Were the Confirmation Signal

The trade required visible stock builds. A surplus that exists only in forecasts may not affect a short-dated futures contract. Rising OECD and commercial inventories would provide evidence that excess barrels were moving from models into storage.

## Catalysts

<ResearchPanel label="Investment case" title="What needed to happen before expiry">

- Further OPEC+ production restoration
- Continued US and other non-OPEC output growth
- Weak Chinese refinery demand relative to imports
- Rising OECD and US commercial inventories
- Cooling geopolitical tensions
- A more persistent move into contango

</ResearchPanel>

## Principal Risks

<ResearchPanel label="Downside analysis" title="Why short oil is structurally dangerous">

- Military conflict or sanctions removing supply
- OPEC+ pausing or reversing output increases
- Unexpected demand strength
- Short covering in a crowded bearish market
- Refinery disruptions tightening product markets
- A futures stop so wide that position sizing becomes inefficient

</ResearchPanel>

The original $86 stop was not well matched to a target only $3–6 below entry. A more disciplined structure would have used a tighter risk threshold, an option spread, or a calendar expression that isolated the expected inventory build.

---

## Outcome and Reflection

EIA spot data provide a transparent benchmark, although they are not identical to the January ICE futures settlement.

Brent averaged $63.80 in November 2025 and $62.54 in December. The weekly spot series reached $60.83 in the week ending 19 December, close to but above the stated target range. It then reversed: $62.18 in the first week of January, $67.00 by mid-month and $70.43 in the final week.

<ResearchPanel label="Retrospective" title="The target was not achieved">

The bearish physical thesis did not translate into the required price move before the contract horizon ended. The trade should therefore be recorded as **unsuccessful**.

The key error was using a short-dated outright future to express a slower balance-sheet view. The position was vulnerable to timing, geopolitics and changes in OPEC behaviour before inventories could do the analytical work.

A better expression would have been a defined-risk put spread, a deferred calendar spread or a conditional short entered only after inventory and curve data confirmed the surplus.

</ResearchPanel>
