## Executive Summary

<ResearchPanel label="Executive summary" title="Three distinct routes to commodity exposure">

On 23 October 2025, I presented three bullish ideas linked to commodity and industrial cycles: Sika, Clean Harbors and uranium. The common theme was not a single macro forecast. It was the possibility that broad sector pessimism was obscuring more durable, company-specific or supply-chain-specific sources of value.

Sika offered exposure to European renovation, specialty construction materials and acquisition synergies. Clean Harbors offered exposure to regulated, non-discretionary environmental services rather than a politically fragile clean-energy narrative. Uranium offered a longer-duration thesis based on nuclear growth, fuel-cycle concentration and the need for utilities to secure future supply.

Subsequent evidence was strongest for Clean Harbors, mixed but operationally constructive for Sika, and still unresolved for uranium because the original horizon was multi-year.

</ResearchPanel>

## 1. Sika — Premium Quality, Cyclical End Markets

### Original Thesis

Sika supplies systems and products used in bonding, sealing, reinforcing and protecting buildings and industrial assets. The investment case rested on three ideas.

First, the European Union's Renovation Wave targeted the renovation of 35 million buildings by 2030 and sought at least to double the annual rate of energy renovation. A larger renovation market should support demand for waterproofing, insulation, repair and structural-strengthening products.

Second, Sika's local production footprint reduced the degree to which tariff volatility translated directly into import costs. This mattered in a period when trade policy was becoming less predictable.

Third, the integration of MBCC created opportunities in procurement, distribution, cross-selling and margin improvement. The original thesis assumed that these synergies could offset weak construction markets and help justify a premium valuation.

### Variant Perception

The differentiated view was that Sika should not be analysed only as a high-multiple construction proxy. Its product intensity, geographic reach and integration opportunities could allow it to outgrow muted end markets.

### Risks

The most important risk was valuation. A premium multiple leaves little room for weak organic growth, currency pressure or delayed synergies. Construction exposure also meant that even a high-quality business could struggle if residential and commercial activity remained subdued.

### Subsequent Evidence

Sika's 2025 results supported the operational-quality argument but not an uncomplicated growth story. Sales increased 0.6% in local currencies, while reported sales fell 4.8% to CHF 11.2 billion because of foreign-exchange effects. Organic growth was negative 0.4%, although it was positive excluding the Chinese construction business. The material margin increased to 54.9%, and the adjusted EBITDA margin was 19.2% after excluding Fast Forward restructuring costs.

This was a **partial validation**. Sika gained share and protected margins in difficult markets, but the original thesis underestimated the drag from weak construction activity and currencies. Without a precisely recorded entry price and target, the idea cannot be presented as a measured trading success.

## 2. Clean Harbors — Essential Infrastructure, Not a Theme Trade

### Original Thesis

Clean Harbors provides hazardous-waste collection, treatment, disposal, recycling and industrial services across North America.

The core insight was that the market risked grouping the company with politically exposed environmental themes. In reality, much of its business was regulated and non-discretionary. Industrial facilities, energy producers and laboratories must continue to manage hazardous materials regardless of changing attitudes toward subsidies or renewable investment.

The company also had cyclical upside. A recovery in US manufacturing, project activity or energy production could raise waste volumes, while scarce incineration and landfill assets supported pricing power.

### Variant Perception

The business was better understood as essential industrial infrastructure with environmental characteristics than as a speculative clean-technology exposure.

### Risks

The thesis was exposed to industrial weakness, acquisition execution, environmental liabilities, base-oil pricing in Safety-Kleen and the capital intensity of maintaining specialised assets.

### Subsequent Evidence

The operating results validated the resilience thesis. Clean Harbors reported record 2025 revenue of $6.03 billion, adjusted EBITDA of $1.17 billion and record adjusted free cash flow of $509 million. Environmental Services expanded its adjusted EBITDA margin despite muted industrial conditions.

The company then reported record first-quarter 2026 revenue of $1.46 billion and 6% growth in adjusted EBITDA to $247.9 million, while raising its full-year guidance.

This was the clearest fundamental success in the report. The company demonstrated that regulated environmental services could continue to grow even without a broad industrial boom.

## 3. Uranium — A Long-Duration Contracting Thesis

### Original Thesis

The uranium view was based on rising nuclear generation, constrained development timelines and the strategic importance of secure fuel supply.

The strongest version of the thesis was not that utilities were about to run out of uranium immediately. It was that reactor requirements were likely to grow faster than dependable, geopolitically diversified supply unless higher prices supported new mines, restarts and fuel-cycle investment.

World Nuclear Association projections published in 2025 estimated reactor requirements of approximately 68,920 tonnes of uranium in 2025, rising to just over 150,000 tonnes by 2040 in its reference scenario.

US contracting data added nuance. At the end of 2024, US reactor operators had most of their anticipated 2025–2027 requirements covered, but unfilled requirements rose materially from 2028 onward. The original presentation therefore overstated the immediacy of a "contracting cliff." The more defensible conclusion was that future coverage became progressively thinner rather than that near-term fuel security was absent.

### Inventory Correction

The initial work also described inventories as depleted. US Energy Information Administration data showed the opposite for the United States: total commercial uranium inventories increased to 167 million pounds U3O8 equivalent at the end of 2024.

That did not eliminate the strategic thesis. Inventories can provide a bridge, but they do not replace long-term production, conversion and enrichment capacity. It did mean that the case should focus on future contracting and supply concentration rather than an immediate physical shortage.

### Risks

- Mine restarts and new supply arriving faster than expected
- Inventory drawdowns delaying contracting urgency
- Slower reactor construction or extended shutdowns
- Substitution among suppliers and fuel-cycle technologies
- Policy changes that reopen access to Russian material

### Current Assessment

The uranium thesis remains **open rather than proven**. The original horizon was multi-year, and the verified evidence supports long-run demand growth and a widening future contracting gap. It does not support claiming that a near-term shortage was inevitable.

## Outcome and Reflection

<ResearchPanel label="Retrospective" title="What the report demonstrated">

- **Sika:** operational quality and margin resilience were visible, but weak end markets and currencies limited growth. Partial validation.
- **Clean Harbors:** essential-service resilience and pricing power were strongly validated by record revenue, EBITDA and free cash flow.
- **Uranium:** the structural thesis remains plausible, but the original analysis overstated near-term inventory and contracting stress.

The main process lesson was that thematic exposure is not enough. Each idea needed a defined entry price, target and invalidation condition. Their absence makes the original work useful as research, but less useful as a verifiable track record.

</ResearchPanel>
