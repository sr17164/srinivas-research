## Executive Summary

<ResearchPanel label="Executive summary" title="A higher target was reached, but not for the expected reason alone">

On 5 February 2026, after the original aluminium target had been reached, I maintained a bullish view and recommended buying consolidation between $3,000 and $3,100 per tonne. The new target was $3,450–3,600 over three to five months, with a stop below $2,850.

The re-underwriting focused on China's 45 million-tonne production ceiling, LME stocks near 495,000 tonnes, limited Western smelter flexibility and the possibility of copper-to-aluminium substitution.

The target was reached during the stated horizon. By late May, Reuters reported LME aluminium around $3,670 after severe Gulf supply disruption. The price outcome was successful, but the path included an unanticipated geopolitical shock. The result therefore validates the exposure to supply convexity more strongly than it validates every element of the original base case.

</ResearchPanel>

## Original Thesis and Update

The November 2025 recommendation targeted $2,950–3,050 and had already worked by early February. A new long required more than repeating the same narrative.

The update argued that the market had not merely completed a cyclical rebound. It was beginning to recognise a supply system with limited spare capacity:

- China was operating close to its administrative ceiling.
- LME inventories remained low.
- Western smelter restarts were slow and power-sensitive.
- Copper near elevated levels encouraged substitution in selected applications.
- European carbon policy altered regional premiums and trade flows.

## What Changed

### 1. The First Target Had Been Reached Without a Large Inventory Rebuild

The original thesis expected tight visible stocks to support a move through $3,000. By February, the price had risen while the presentation still recorded LME inventories near 495,000 tonnes.

This suggested that the move had not yet triggered a major supply response. The market could therefore remain sensitive to additional disruption.

### 2. China's Ceiling Looked More Binding

China's role as swing producer had diminished as annualised output approached the approximately 45 million-tonne ceiling.

The original work treated the ceiling as fixed. In reality, policy implementation, capacity swaps and exemptions could alter effective output. The thesis was that marginal flexibility had narrowed, not that production could never exceed a precise number.

### 3. Smelter Supply Was Power-Constrained

Primary aluminium requires continuous electricity. Potlines are costly to stop and slow to restart, making the industry vulnerable to power shortages and contract disputes.

The presentation's claim that AI data centres had replaced inventory as the primary price setter was too sweeping. The defensible point was that competition for reliable power and higher electricity costs made Western restarts less elastic.

### 4. Substitution Added Incremental Demand

High copper prices can encourage aluminium use in transmission and selected electrical applications. The original estimate of 800,000–850,000 tonnes of additional 2026 demand was not independently verified and has been removed.

Substitution was best treated as an incremental tailwind, not the central balance assumption.

### 5. Carbon Policy Changed Regional Economics

The European Union's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism increased the importance of embedded emissions and could support premiums for lower-carbon aluminium. It did not mechanically remove standard LME-grade metal from the market, but it could reshape trade flows and regional value.

## Outcome and Reflection

The target range was reached within the stated horizon. Reuters reported LME aluminium near $3,670/t on 27 May 2026, after the Gulf region suffered severe production and shipping disruption. The International Aluminium Institute estimated that Gulf producers had supplied about 6.5 million tonnes in 2025, or 9% of global production, while accounting for a larger share of US and EU imports.

On 17 July, the LME official three-month aluminium bid and offer were $3,110 and $3,112/t, respectively.

<ResearchPanel label="Retrospective" title="Price target achieved; causal attribution was mixed">

The recommendation should be recorded as **successful on price and horizon**. The $3,450–3,600 target was reached within roughly four months.

However, the decisive acceleration came from a geopolitical supply shock that was not the explicit base case. The original thesis correctly identified limited supply flexibility and therefore owned useful convexity, but it should not claim to have forecast the conflict.

The later retracement reinforces the need for predefined profit-taking. Reaching a target is not the same as permanently establishing a new equilibrium price.

</ResearchPanel>
