## Executive Summary

<ResearchPanel label="Executive summary" title="A correct supply-cycle thesis with an implementation mismatch">

On 20 November 2025, I presented a bullish view on live cattle at approximately 221.27 cents per hundredweight. The thesis was built on a prolonged contraction in the US cattle herd, slow biological replenishment and resilient beef demand. The original presentation recommended December 2025 and February 2026 futures and used $248.50/cwt as a target.

The fundamental thesis was validated. USDA data later showed 86.2 million cattle and calves on US farms at the start of 2026, with beef cows down 1%, the calf crop down 2% and cattle on feed down 3%.

Nearby futures exceeded $248.50 in June 2026. However, that occurred after the named December and February contracts had expired. The research direction was correct, but the selected maturities were poorly aligned with the slow cattle-cycle catalyst unless the position was explicitly rolled.

</ResearchPanel>

## Market Background

Cattle differ from storable commodities. A price increase cannot quickly produce more finished animals.

Rebuilding begins when producers retain heifers rather than send them to slaughter. That initially reduces beef supply further. The retained animals must mature, breed and produce calves, which then require additional time to reach market weight. The supply response is therefore measured in years rather than weeks.

This biological lag was the foundation of the thesis.

---

## Investment Thesis

### 1. The Herd Was Near a Generational Low

USDA's March 2025 long-term analysis projected that US cattle inventories would bottom near 86 million head in 2025, the lowest level since 1951. The January 2026 survey later reported 86.2 million head.

The level alone was not sufficient; the composition mattered. Beef cows were 27.6 million, down 1%, while the 2025 calf crop was estimated at 32.9 million, down 2%. Those figures constrained the number of animals available to enter the production chain.

### 2. The Supply Response Was Delayed

High feeder and finished-cattle prices improve the incentive to rebuild, but drought, feed availability and producer balance sheets affect the decision to retain heifers.

Even when retention begins, it can tighten near-term slaughter supply before increasing future output. This created a convex setup: evidence of rebuilding could be bullish initially and bearish only later.

### 3. Demand Had Been Resilient

Consumers continued buying beef despite high retail prices, limiting the degree of demand destruction. The thesis assumed that household substitution toward poultry would be gradual rather than immediate.

Demand resilience was a necessary condition. A small herd does not guarantee higher futures prices if recession or food inflation causes consumption to contract sharply.

### 4. Imports Could Cushion, Not Eliminate, Tightness

Higher US prices attract beef imports and can reduce exports. USDA's longer-term work expected imports to peak as domestic production remained constrained.

The original presentation was too dismissive of this channel. Imported beef cannot instantly replace every domestic cut or quality specification, but trade is a meaningful balancing mechanism and should be included in the bear case.

## Principal Risks

<ResearchPanel label="Downside analysis" title="What could weaken the thesis">

- Demand destruction at record retail prices
- Larger beef imports
- Faster herd rebuilding than expected
- Lower feed costs accelerating weight gain and supply
- Currency moves that weaken export demand
- Contract-specific basis and roll risk

## Outcome and Reflection

The January 2026 USDA inventory report confirmed the central supply thesis: total cattle inventory fell to 86.2 million head, beef cows declined and the calf crop contracted.

Public nearby-futures data show live cattle trading above the 248.50 target during June 2026, including closes around 249–255 and a late-June high above 258. Prices later retreated sharply toward the mid-220s by 17 July.

<ResearchPanel label="Retrospective" title="Fundamental success, maturity-selection problem">

The directional thesis was **successful on a six-to-nine-month continuous-market basis** because the target was exceeded in June.

It was not a clean success for the specifically named December 2025 and February 2026 contracts, which expired before the target was reached. Capturing the move would have required an explicit roll into later maturities.

This distinction matters. A commodity thesis can be right while the chosen contract is wrong. Future reports should state the exact maturity, roll rule, carry cost and whether the target applies to that contract or to a continuous nearby benchmark.

</ResearchPanel>
