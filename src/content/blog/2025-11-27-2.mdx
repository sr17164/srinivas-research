## Executive Summary

<ResearchPanel label="Executive summary" title="A tightness thesis that reached its target">

On 27 November 2025, I recommended buying LME three-month aluminium at approximately $2,861 per tonne. The target was $2,950–3,050 over six to twelve weeks.

The thesis centred on China's approximately 45 million-tonne annual output ceiling, LME inventories near 544,000 tonnes, limited Western swing capacity and durable demand from grids, transport and solar infrastructure.

By the 5 February 2026 follow-up presentation, LME three-month aluminium was recorded around $3,050–3,120. The original target had therefore been reached near the end of the intended window. The call was successful, although several supporting claims—especially precise demand from artificial-intelligence data centres and the description of inventories as a direct measure of global scarcity—were too strong.

</ResearchPanel>

## Market Background

Aluminium was balancing weak cyclical sentiment against a supply system with limited flexibility.

China produced the majority of global primary aluminium and was approaching an administrative ceiling near 45 million tonnes per year. Outside China, high power costs and long restart timelines limited the ability of idled smelters to respond quickly.

Visible LME stocks were low relative to earlier periods, while investment funds had built substantial long exposure. The setup combined physical tightness with the risk of crowded positioning.

---

## Investment Thesis

### 1. China's Output Ceiling Reduced Swing Capacity

The 45 million-tonne ceiling was the centre of the thesis. Chinese annualised output was already near 44 million tonnes, leaving less room for the country to respond to higher prices as the global swing producer.

The ceiling was not an absolute physical limit. Capacity swaps, efficiency improvements and policy exemptions could alter effective production. It nevertheless reduced expected supply elasticity.

### 2. Visible Inventories Were Low

The original work recorded LME inventories near 543,725 tonnes, below early-2025 levels and historical norms.

Low exchange stocks increased sensitivity to disruptions and nearby demand. They did not prove that total global stocks were exhausted: metal can be held off warrant, in producer systems or in regional warehouses. The defensible conclusion was that **immediately deliverable LME availability was limited**.

### 3. Western Supply Could Not Restart Quickly

Primary aluminium smelting is power-intensive. High electricity costs, closure risk and long potline restart periods constrained short-term supply in Europe, the United States and Australia.

The original presentation described data centres as directly competing with smelters for power. That can occur in particular grids, but the broad claim was not adequately quantified. The stronger point was that power availability and long-term electricity contracts were increasingly important constraints on smelter economics.

### 4. Infrastructure Demand Provided Support

Grid expansion, electric vehicles, solar installations and lightweight transport increased aluminium intensity. Copper-to-aluminium substitution added potential demand where conductivity, weight and cost allowed.

This demand was structural but not immune to the cycle. Construction and manufacturing remained large end markets, so a global slowdown could overwhelm energy-transition growth in the short run.

### 5. Positioning Could Accelerate the Move

Large fund-long positions increased the probability of a breakout if physical data remained supportive. They also increased liquidation risk if inventories rose or macro conditions deteriorated.

The thesis therefore required both physical confirmation and disciplined risk management.

## Outcome and Reflection

The 5 February 2026 update recorded LME three-month aluminium at approximately $3,050–3,120/t. That placed the market at or above the original $2,950–3,050 target approximately ten weeks after publication.

<ResearchPanel label="Retrospective" title="The original target was reached">

The trade should be recorded as **successful** on its stated objective and horizon.

The strongest parts of the original analysis were the focus on China's production ceiling, low visible inventory and limited short-term supply response. The weakest parts were the use of exchange stocks as a proxy for the entire market and unverified precision around AI and substitution demand.

The follow-up research correctly required re-underwriting rather than mechanically extending the first target. Once aluminium reached the original objective, the relevant question became whether the same factors justified a much higher second target.

</ResearchPanel>
