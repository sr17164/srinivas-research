## Executive Summary

<ResearchPanel label="Executive summary" title="A correct catalyst, reached much faster than expected">

On 6 November 2025, I presented a bullish view on Henry Hub natural gas at approximately $3.40 per million British thermal units. The thesis combined a structural rise in LNG feed-gas demand with cyclical winter heating demand, slower production growth and a lower gas-directed rig count. The target was $4.50–5.00 over six to nine months.

The target was reached within weeks rather than months. EIA spot data show Henry Hub averaging $4.29 in the week ending 28 November and $4.97 in the week ending 5 December. Prices then fell back before an extraordinary weather-driven spike in January 2026.

The thesis was successful as a tactical call. The process lesson was that a commodity target should be paired with an explicit take-profit rule: maintaining the position mechanically for the full six-to-nine-month horizon would have surrendered much of the gain.

</ResearchPanel>

## Market Background

US natural gas was transitioning from a largely domestic commodity into a market increasingly linked to global gas balances.

The original work recorded LNG exports near 14.3 billion cubic feet per day, around 10% higher year on year, storage near 3.8 trillion cubic feet and approximately 110 gas-directed rigs. These figures were intended to capture a market with substantial inventories but rising structural demand and less aggressive upstream investment.

The central question was whether LNG growth and winter consumption could tighten the domestic balance before producers responded.

---

## Investment Thesis

### 1. LNG Was Raising the Demand Floor

New liquefaction capacity increased the amount of US gas capable of reaching global markets. That mattered because it linked Henry Hub more closely to European and Asian price signals and converted export infrastructure into a persistent source of domestic demand.

The EIA's November 2025 outlook subsequently projected gross US LNG exports of 15 Bcf/d in 2025 and 16 Bcf/d in 2026. This broadly supported the structural direction of the original thesis.

### 2. Winter Weather Created Convexity

Heating demand is highly nonlinear. A modestly cold forecast can tighten balances, while a severe cold event can produce large regional and daily price spikes because pipeline and storage constraints become binding.

The original call did not require an extreme event, but colder-than-normal conditions represented the clearest near-term catalyst.

### 3. Supply Growth Was Less Certain

A lower rig count and producer capital discipline reduced confidence that production would immediately offset stronger demand. The thesis assumed that output growth would remain slow enough for inventories to normalise.

This was not the same as claiming that the United States lacked gas resources. Henry Hub supply is elastic over time; the bullish opportunity existed in the lag between higher demand and the drilling response.

### 4. Valuation Provided Asymmetry

At approximately $3.40, the price was well below the original target and near the level at which some producers faced less attractive drilling economics. The downside was still meaningful if winter proved mild, but the combination of seasonality and LNG growth created favourable upside asymmetry.

## Catalysts

<ResearchPanel label="Investment case" title="What could tighten the balance">

- Colder-than-normal winter weather
- New LNG trains ramping feed-gas demand
- Falling or stable gas-directed rig counts
- Storage moving toward seasonal norms
- Stronger industrial and power-sector demand
- High international gas prices preserving export margins

## Principal Risks

<ResearchPanel label="Downside analysis" title="What could invalidate the long">

- A mild winter
- Rapid production growth
- Storage remaining materially above seasonal norms
- LNG outages or commissioning delays
- Pipeline constraints limiting feed-gas demand
- Demand destruction after a rapid price increase

## Outcome and Reflection

The weekly EIA Henry Hub spot series rose from $3.54 in the week ending 7 November to $4.29 by 28 November and $4.97 by 5 December. The original $4.50–5.00 target was therefore reached approximately one month after publication.

Prices then declined to $3.45 in the final week of December. In January, an extreme weather event produced weekly averages above $12 and daily prints as high as $30.72, before the market rapidly returned toward $3 in February and March.

<ResearchPanel label="Retrospective" title="Successful view, incomplete exit framework">

The trade achieved its target and should be recorded as **successful** on the original price objective.

The more important lesson concerns exit discipline. The six-to-nine-month horizon described how long the structural thesis could develop; it should not have required holding after the target was reached. Natural gas is highly mean-reverting and sensitive to weather. A predefined scale-out or trailing stop would have converted a correct thesis into a more robust process.

The January spike should not be credited to the November research as though it had been precisely forecast. It was an extreme weather realisation that demonstrated the convexity of the market, not the base case.

</ResearchPanel>
