## Executive Summary

<ResearchPanel label="Executive summary" title="A contracting thesis, not an immediate shortage claim">

On 20 November 2025, I presented a bullish multi-year view on uranium. The original work recorded spot uranium around $76–80 per pound and targeted a move above $100 as utility contracting, reactor growth and supply-chain concentration tightened the market.

The structural conclusion remains defensible, but several original figures required correction. US utilities were not 70% uncovered for 2027–2028, and US commercial uranium inventories were rising rather than depleted. The stronger thesis is that coverage declines materially from 2028 onward, while global reactor requirements could more than double by 2040 and new mine and fuel-cycle capacity requires long lead times.

Because the horizon was 12–36 months and no independently verified closing target has been established for this review, the position remains open rather than classified as a success or failure.

</ResearchPanel>

## Market Background

Uranium is a small commodity market embedded in a much larger electricity system. Fuel is a relatively modest share of a nuclear plant's total operating cost, while reliability and regulatory approval are critical. This can make utility demand less price-sensitive than demand in many industrial commodities.

At the same time, the market is not defined only by mined U3O8. Conversion, enrichment and fuel fabrication can become bottlenecks, and geopolitical restrictions can alter the value of material at different stages of the cycle.

## Investment Thesis

### 1. Reactor Requirements Were Set to Rise

The World Nuclear Association's 2025 Nuclear Fuel Report estimated global reactor requirements of approximately 68,920 tonnes of uranium in 2025. Its reference scenario projected just over 150,000 tonnes by 2040, with an upper scenario above 204,000 tonnes.

The thesis did not require the upper case. Even the reference path implied a substantial increase in dependable annual supply.

### 2. The Contracting Gap Was Deferred, Not Immediate

The original presentation claimed that roughly 70% of 2027–2028 utility needs were uncovered. US EIA data do not support that statement.

At the end of 2024, US reactor operators reported maximum contracted deliveries of 41.9 million pounds for 2027 against maximum anticipated requirements of 45.3 million pounds. For 2028, contracted deliveries were 30.2 million pounds against requirements of 41.8 million pounds. The unfilled share therefore rose sharply in 2028, but it was nowhere near 70% across both years.

The gap widened more materially later: unfilled requirements were 20.5 million pounds in 2030, 32.6 million in 2031 and 33.8 million in 2032.

This correction improves the analysis. The market did not face an immediate wall in 2026; it faced a progressively larger procurement requirement that could encourage earlier contracting.

### 3. Inventories Were a Buffer

US commercial uranium inventories reached approximately 167 million pounds U3O8 equivalent at the end of 2024, up 6% year on year. Reactor-owner inventories rose to about 126 million pounds.

The original description of inventories as broadly depleted was therefore inaccurate for the United States.

Inventories still do not remove the long-term problem. They can delay purchases, smooth disruptions and reduce near-term price sensitivity, but drawing them down is not a permanent source of supply. Their existence means the timing of the bull case depends heavily on utility procurement behaviour.

### 4. Supply and Fuel-Cycle Capacity Were Concentrated

Kazakhstan remained a dominant mine supplier, while Russia retained an important role in conversion and enrichment. The US prohibition on Russian low-enriched uranium imports, subject to temporary waivers, demonstrated how policy could fragment a previously global fuel cycle.

New mines, restarts and enrichment capacity require capital, permitting, technical expertise and time. Higher prices may ultimately solve the shortage by encouraging supply, but that response is slow.

### 5. Data-Centre Demand Strengthened the Nuclear Narrative

Electricity demand from data centres increased interest in reliable, low-carbon baseload generation. Corporate agreements to support existing or restarted nuclear capacity improved the strategic case for reactor life extensions and new builds.

This was a useful demand catalyst, but the original work overstated the idea that technology companies were directly price-insensitive buyers of uranium. Most buy electricity, not uranium. The effect is indirect through reactor economics and capacity decisions.

## Catalysts

<ResearchPanel label="Investment case" title="What could accelerate the thesis">

- Larger long-term utility contracting volumes
- Reactor restarts and life extensions
- New nuclear build commitments
- Delays or cost overruns at major mines
- Restrictions on Russian conversion or enrichment services
- Higher term prices relative to spot
- Strategic inventory accumulation by governments or utilities

</ResearchPanel>

## Principal Risks

<ResearchPanel label="Downside analysis" title="Why the timing can remain difficult">

- Large inventories delaying utility purchases
- Faster Kazakh, Canadian or African supply growth
- Mine restarts responding to higher prices
- Reactor delays, closures or cost overruns
- Policy waivers preserving access to Russian fuel services
- Equity valuations outrunning the underlying commodity

</ResearchPanel>

## Outcome and Reflection

<ResearchPanel label="Retrospective" title="The horizon remains open">

The verified data support a long-run requirement for more uranium and fuel-cycle capacity. They do not support the original claim of an immediate contracting panic or depleted US inventories.

The position should therefore be classified as **open**. A disciplined future review should assess the $100 target against a clearly specified spot or term benchmark, track annual contracting volumes and define whether the thesis is invalidated by new supply, weaker reactor growth or sustained inventory accumulation.

The main lesson is that a structural shortage thesis must distinguish between resources in the ground, annual mine production, inventories, contracted deliveries and each stage of the fuel cycle. Combining them into one headline deficit creates false precision.

</ResearchPanel>
