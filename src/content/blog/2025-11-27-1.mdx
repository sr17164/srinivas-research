## Executive Summary

<ResearchPanel label="Executive summary" title="A plausible scarcity story that failed as a tactical trade">

On 27 November 2025, I recommended a tactical long in ICE Arabica coffee after a tariff-related correction. The original work recorded a reference range of approximately 395–405 cents per pound, certified stocks of 398,645 bags and a target of 420–440 cents over four to eight weeks.

The thesis combined low visible inventory with Brazilian weather risk and the possibility that the tariff-driven fall had created a cleaner entry. The market moved in the opposite direction. The monthly average for the nearby ICE contract fell from 408.0 cents in November to 376.3 in December and 356.3 in January 2026. The target was not achieved.

The failure showed that low stocks can amplify upside after a supply shock, but they do not guarantee that the shock will occur. Policy normalisation, improving supply expectations and crowded positioning outweighed the weather-risk narrative.

</ResearchPanel>

## Market Background

Coffee entered late 2025 after a powerful rally. Weather problems, tight inventories and trade-policy uncertainty had already driven Arabica to historically elevated levels.

That context created two competing interpretations. The bullish view saw the pullback as an entry into a structurally tight market. The bearish view saw a crowded, expensive commodity whose risk premium could fall quickly if rainfall or trade flows improved.

The original thesis underestimated how much scarcity was already priced.

---

## Investment Thesis

### 1. Visible Stocks Were Low

The original presentation recorded ICE-certified stocks of 398,645 bags. Low exchange stocks reduce the buffer available against a crop or logistics disruption and can magnify futures squeezes.

However, certified stocks are not the same as total global inventories. Coffee can sit outside the exchange system, and high prices can encourage previously uncertified material to enter deliverable channels. Exchange stocks should therefore be treated as a market-structure indicator, not a complete supply measure.

### 2. Brazilian Weather Created Optionality

Brazil is central to global Arabica supply. The original research highlighted a high probability of La Niña conditions and the possibility of drought stress during a sensitive period for the 2026/27 crop.

Weather risk was the strongest catalyst because it could change yield expectations quickly. It was also the weakest forecasting variable: rainfall models and crop assessments can reverse from week to week.

### 3. The Tariff Reversal Created an Apparent Entry

The removal of US tariffs on Brazilian green coffee contributed to a sharp correction around 20 November. The thesis interpreted this as a one-off policy adjustment that improved entry without changing the underlying crop balance.

In retrospect, the policy move was more important. It eased a distortion in trade flows at a time when speculative positioning and high prices were vulnerable.

### 4. Demand Was Relatively Inelastic, Not Immune

Coffee consumption is habitual, but roasters can alter blends, reduce promotions or substitute Robusta when Arabica becomes exceptionally expensive. The original work was too confident that demand would remain unchanged.

At high prices, elasticity can appear through the supply chain even if end consumers continue buying coffee.

## Outcome and Reflection

INSEE's nearby ICE Arabica series averaged 408.0 cents/lb in November 2025, 376.3 in December and 356.3 in January 2026. It fell further to 296.4 in February and remained near 300 through April.

The International Coffee Organization reported that its broader composite indicator averaged 248.90 cents/lb in June 2026 after reaching a nearly two-year low earlier in the month. Arabica exports in the twelve months to May were lower year on year, but that did not prevent a major price decline.

<ResearchPanel label="Retrospective" title="The trade failed">

The 420–440 target was not achieved within the four-to-eight-week horizon. The call should be recorded as **unsuccessful**.

The central analytical mistake was treating low visible stocks as a directional signal rather than a conditional amplifier. Stocks increased the likely size of a rally **if** weather deteriorated; they did not establish the probability of that weather outcome.

A better structure would have used options to isolate weather convexity or waited for crop and rainfall data to confirm deterioration before entering an outright long.

</ResearchPanel>
