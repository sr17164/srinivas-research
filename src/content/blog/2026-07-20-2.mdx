## Executive Summary

<ResearchPanel label="Current view" title="Structurally bullish, tactically neutral">

I am neutral copper over the next three to six months. The structural case remains compelling: new mines are slow to permit and build, concentrate supply is tight, and grid expansion, electrification and data-centre investment are increasing copper intensity.

The near-term risk-reward is less attractive. LME three-month copper closed at approximately **$13,526/t on 17 July 2026**, after prices briefly exceeded $14,500/t earlier in the year. At the same time, visible stocks have increased and the United States has absorbed an unusually large share of exchange inventory because tariff uncertainty distorted regional flows.

At current prices, the market already discounts significant future scarcity. I would avoid chasing the rally and wait for either a correction towards **$11,750–12,500/t** or renewed evidence that inventories and physical availability are tightening outside the United States.

## Positioning

<ResearchPanel label="Trade framework" title="Wait for a better asymmetry" compact>

- **Reference price:** approximately $13,526/t for LME three-month copper on 17 July 2026.
- **Current stance:** neutral over three to six months; constructive over a multi-year horizon.
- **Preferred entry zone:** $11,750–12,500/t, provided the structural thesis remains intact.
- **Upside trigger:** broad ex-US inventory draws, stronger Chinese imports and firmer physical premiums.
- **Downside trigger:** inventory growth across regions alongside weaker manufacturing demand.
- **Invalidation of neutrality:** a major mine disruption or clear evidence that available refined metal is tightening despite high prices.

## Investment Thesis

### 1. The Structural Supply Gap Remains the Long-Term Anchor

The IEA's 2026 Critical Minerals Outlook still projects a copper supply gap of approximately **25% by 2035** under its base-case project pipeline. The deficit narrowed from the prior year's estimate as projects advanced in the DRC and Zambia, but it remains unusually large for a globally traded base metal.

Copper also has a broad and difficult-to-substitute demand base. Grid reinforcement, renewable generation, electric transport, industrial electrification and data centres all require conductive metal. These uses support a higher long-run incentive price and reduce the probability of a sustained collapse in investment.

A strong secular case, however, does not make every entry price attractive.

### 2. Current Prices Already Capitalise Much of the Scarcity Narrative

Copper briefly traded above **$14,500/t intraday in January 2026**, having first crossed $12,000/t only in December 2025. The LME three-month close on 17 July remained around $13,526/t.

At those levels, the market is not waiting for a future shortage; it is already discounting one. High prices can persist when inventories are inaccessible or mine disruptions intensify, but the valuation leaves less protection against slower demand, successful project ramp-ups or speculative liquidation.

The neutral view is therefore primarily about entry price and expected return, not disagreement with the long-term demand thesis.

### 3. Visible Stocks Are High, but Their Location Distorts the Signal

Global visible copper stocks have risen materially in 2026. A large share of the increase has accumulated in the United States as tariff uncertainty pulled metal towards COMEX warehouses and changed trade incentives.

This makes the headline total difficult to interpret. Metal in a US warehouse is not automatically available to a Chinese or European consumer at the same economics, specification and timing. Conversely, the distortion can reverse: a policy change may release inventory back into the international market and remove an artificial source of tightness elsewhere.

The correct conclusion is not that inventory is irrelevant. It is that **broad-based stock movements across LME, SHFE, COMEX and bonded warehouses** matter more than any single headline number.

### 4. Concentrate Tightness Supports the Structural Case, but Smelter Stress Is Not a Simple Bullish Signal

The IEA reports that benchmark copper treatment and refining charges were settled at **$0/t in 2026**, with spot charges remaining negative. This reflects tight concentrate availability and rapid smelter-capacity growth, particularly in China.

For miners, this is evidence that raw-material supply remains constrained. For the integrated copper balance, the signal is more nuanced. Smelter financial stress can curtail refined output, but high capacity and policy support can also keep plants operating despite poor standalone economics.

Treatment charges should therefore be monitored alongside refined production and physical premiums rather than used as a standalone price signal.

### 5. Major Producers Show Both Constraint and Adaptability

The supply story is not uniformly bearish or bullish. BHP produced roughly **2 million tonnes of copper for a second consecutive year** in FY26, supported by strong performance at Escondida. That demonstrates that operational improvement and capital investment can offset lower grades at high-quality assets.

At the same time, BHP's March review showed lower Escondida feed grades and operational difficulties at Spence, illustrating the geological and processing challenges embedded in mature assets. The producer response is real, but it is capital-intensive, slow and uneven.

This mixed evidence supports neutrality: the market faces a long-term project challenge, but near-term supply is not collapsing across the board.

### 6. Near-Term Demand Has More Price Elasticity Than the Structural Narrative Suggests

Grid and digital-infrastructure demand are durable, yet high prices can still change purchasing behaviour. Fabricators can destock, defer orders, increase scrap use or redesign products. Slower construction and manufacturing activity can also offset strength in strategic sectors over shorter periods.

The IEA's own analysis highlights that record prices were amplified by lower interest rates, a softer dollar, investor demand for physical assets and financial speculation. Those supports can reverse more quickly than mine supply or grid demand.

A neutral position therefore preserves exposure to the structural thesis without assuming that every driver remains favourable over the next quarter or two.

## Catalysts

<ResearchPanel label="What could turn the view bullish" title="Evidence required before chasing the rally">

- Sustained declines in LME, SHFE, COMEX and bonded inventories
- Stronger Chinese refined-copper imports and physical premiums
- Further disruptions at major mines
- Refined-output curtailments caused by prolonged smelter stress
- Stronger grid, power and data-centre capital expenditure
- A price correction that restores a larger margin of safety

</ResearchPanel>

## Principal Risks

<ResearchPanel label="What could turn the view bearish" title="Signals that price has moved ahead of demand">

- Continued inventory growth across multiple regions
- Slower Chinese manufacturing and construction demand
- Faster mine ramp-ups in the DRC, Zambia, Peru or Argentina
- Increased scrap availability and substitution
- A stronger US dollar or tighter global financial conditions
- Reversal of tariff-driven stock dislocation, releasing US-held metal back to the global market

</ResearchPanel>

## Conclusion

Copper remains one of the strongest long-duration commodity stories, but a sound secular thesis can still be a poor short-term trade at an aggressive valuation. Record prices, elevated visible stocks and policy-driven regional dislocation reduce the margin of safety, while tight concentrate and a large projected 2035 supply gap prevent a clean bearish view.

The appropriate stance is neutral: retain a constructive multi-year outlook, avoid chasing near $13,500/t and wait for either a meaningful pullback or stronger evidence that physical availability is tightening broadly rather than merely moving between regions.

## Research Methodology

This note combines official benchmark prices, agency supply-demand analysis, producer operating disclosures and inventory-location dynamics. Scenario ranges are judgemental rather than outputs from a proprietary price model. The analysis separates long-run mine scarcity from shorter-run refined-market, inventory and positioning signals.
