## Executive Summary

<ResearchPanel label="Executive summary" title="A regional dislocation, not a demand forecast">

On 24 October 2025, I initiated the short leg of a proposed relative-value trade: short three-month LME zinc and long SHFE zinc. LME on-warrant stocks had fallen to 24,850 tonnes, while Chinese refined-zinc inventories were approximately 162,000 tonnes. The SHFE/LME ratio and physical export economics implied that Chinese metal could be profitably delivered into LME warehouses in Asia.

The expected convergence did not begin immediately. The LME three-month price increased from $3,019.50 per tonne to $3,057.85 by 5 November, producing a paper loss of roughly $38 per tonne on the short. Over the intended four-to-six-week horizon, however, LME inventories rebuilt and the price fell to approximately $2,981 by 20 November.

The market-structure thesis was directionally correct, but the implementation was incomplete because the long SHFE leg was never executed. The realised exposure was therefore an outright LME short rather than a true relative-value position.

</ResearchPanel>

## The Dislocation

Zinc was priced very differently across two connected markets.

LME availability had become exceptionally tight. On-warrant stocks represented less than one day of estimated global consumption, and backwardation reflected the premium buyers were willing to pay for immediately deliverable metal.

China, by contrast, held materially more refined zinc. The original work estimated approximately 162,000 tonnes of inventory and a weak domestic balance. On 24 October, the SHFE/LME ratio was approximately 7.35, and exporting standard high-grade zinc to LME warehouses in Singapore was estimated to generate an arbitrage profit of roughly CNY1,128 per tonne, or around $160.

The variant perception was that the LME squeeze was real but temporary. Once the price premium became large enough to cover freight, financing and warehouse costs, the market itself created the incentive to move metal westward.

---

## Convergence Mechanism

The expected sequence was:

1. LME scarcity pushed nearby prices and backwardation higher.
2. The widening regional premium made Chinese exports profitable.
3. Metal was shipped toward LME-approved Asian warehouses.
4. New warrants increased visible availability.
5. The scarcity premium and backwardation weakened.
6. LME zinc converged toward the better-supplied Chinese market.

An early 2,500-tonne arrival in Singapore provided initial evidence that the mechanism had begun. The investment question was not whether China possessed metal, but whether the flow could arrive before the short squeeze imposed unacceptable mark-to-market losses.

---

## What Went Wrong Initially

The timing was too aggressive.

By 5 November, LME three-month zinc had risen to $3,057.85/t. Rather than collapsing as soon as export economics opened, the scarcity premium intensified. Participants short nearby metal still needed to secure deliverable supply, and physical logistics moved more slowly than the financial market.

LME stocks had increased to approximately 29,500 tonnes, but the rise was too small to break the squeeze. The position therefore experienced a paper loss of approximately $38/t in its first ten days.

This was a useful distinction between a correct mechanism and a tradable catalyst. The existence of an arbitrage does not determine the speed with which ships, customs, warehouse queues and warranting processes resolve it.

---

## Evidence of Convergence

The trade improved as the original holding period matured.

Measured from the recorded entry to 20 November, the short moved approximately $38.50/t in the intended direction, before transaction costs and financing. More importantly, the inventory rebuild confirmed the physical mechanism.

---

## Risks and Invalidation

<ResearchPanel label="Downside analysis" title="What could prevent convergence">

- Chinese exports taking longer than the trade horizon
- LME backwardation remaining large enough to squeeze short positions
- Freight, financing or warehouse costs closing the apparent arbitrage
- Trade restrictions preventing Chinese metal from reaching deliverable locations
- A weaker renminbi or stronger domestic Chinese demand altering the regional spread
- Mine disruptions creating a genuinely tighter global balance

</ResearchPanel>

The appropriate invalidation was not simply a higher LME price. It was evidence that export economics had closed without meaningful warehouse inflows, or that the expected metal could not become LME-deliverable.

---

## Outcome and Reflection

<ResearchPanel label="Retrospective" title="Correct mechanism, incomplete construction">

The thesis worked over the intended four-to-six-week horizon: visible LME inventories increased and the three-month price fell below the original short entry.

However, the position should not be presented as a successful SHFE/LME relative-value trade because the SHFE long was not executed. The implemented position carried outright zinc beta, global-demand risk and US-dollar exposure that the proposed pair was designed to reduce.

The main lesson was that relative-value research and relative-value execution are not the same thing. A strong future version of the trade would specify both legs, hedge ratios, currency treatment, financing costs, delivery locations and a maximum acceptable widening before convergence.

</ResearchPanel>
