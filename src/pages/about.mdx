      <h2>Why It Exists</h2>

      <p>
        SM Research is an independent investment research publication and a
        public record of how I analyse markets.
      </p>

      <p>
        An investment thesis can appear convincing while it remains informal.
        Writing it down creates a higher standard. The opportunity must be
        defined, the assumptions made explicit, the relevant evidence
        separated from narrative and the conditions that would invalidate the
        conclusion stated in advance.
      </p>

      <p>
        The purpose is not simply to publish market opinions or assemble a
        catalogue of successful calls. It is to make the reasoning behind each
        decision inspectable and to create a body of work through which that
        reasoning can be evaluated over time.
      </p>

      <p>
        SM Research is not presented as an investment firm or advisory service.
        It is the research record of an individual analyst developing a more
        disciplined approach to markets, valuation and capital allocation.
      </p>

      <h2>Where It Began</h2>

      <p>
        My interest in markets began with uncertainty and risk transfer rather
        than with stock prices.
      </p>

      <p>
        My mother's side of the family comes from a farming background, so
        agriculture never felt like an abstract component of the economy.
        Livelihoods could be affected by weather, harvest conditions and
        commodity prices that changed materially from one season to the next.
      </p>

      <p>
        Learning how futures markets allow producers to hedge prices changed
        the way I understood finance. Behind a contract may be a farmer
        protecting the value of a harvest, an airline managing fuel exposure or
        a manufacturer securing the cost of a critical input. Markets became
        mechanisms through which businesses transfer, price and manage
        uncertainty.
      </p>

      <p>
        Many of the earliest reports published here originated while I served
        as Commodities Sector Head at Queen's Tower Capital, Imperial College
        London's student-managed investment fund. I researched and prepared the
        underlying commodity work before presenting recommendations through the
        fund's investment process and coordinating coverage across a team of
        five analysts.
      </p>

      <p>
        Those presentations have not been reproduced unchanged. Each has been
        rewritten as a long-form SM Research report, with material figures
        checked against public sources and the original investment case
        separated from information that became available later. Retrospective
        outcomes are presented independently so that subsequent developments do
        not alter the original reasoning.
      </p>

      <h2>Research Approach</h2>

      <p>
        Each report begins with a defined investment question rather than a
        predetermined conclusion. The analysis then considers the market
        structure, supply and demand balance, macroeconomic environment,
        valuation, positioning, catalysts and principal risks relevant to that
        question.
      </p>

      <p>
        A useful thesis should identify more than a directional view. Where the
        evidence permits, it should define an investment horizon, reference
        price, expected catalysts, target framework and the conditions under
        which the view should be reconsidered.
      </p>

      <p>
        Sources are selected with preference for primary data and institutional
        research. Facts, assumptions and interpretations should remain
        distinguishable. Where a number cannot be supported with sufficient
        confidence, it should be qualified or removed rather than presented
        with false precision.
      </p>

      <p>
        Financial models and quantitative methods are used where they improve
        the analysis. They are intended to make assumptions clearer and
        scenarios more testable, not to replace economic reasoning with
        complexity.
      </p>

      <h2>Publication Standard</h2>

      <p>
        Historical reports distinguish between the evidence available when a
        view was formed and information learned later. Outcome sections are
        therefore retrospective evaluations rather than revisions of the
        original record.
      </p>

      <p>
        Where a model can be shared responsibly, the supporting code, data,
        outputs and limitations are published alongside it. Where third-party
        assessment material or proprietary templates cannot be redistributed,
        the methodology and conclusions are documented without exposing the
        underlying file.
      </p>

      <p>
        Material errors are corrected rather than defended. The revised
        commodity-regime project, for example, preserves its original analysis
        while withdrawing conclusions that did not survive a data and
        specification audit. A correction that narrows a result is more useful
        than precision unsupported by the evidence.
      </p>

      <h2>Experience Informing the Research</h2>

      <p>
        I study Economics, Finance & Data Science at Imperial College London.
        Short programmes at Barings, BNP Paribas and Millennium have exposed me
        to high-yield credit, cross-asset markets, market making and systematic
        research. These experiences inform the questions I ask, but they are
        not presented as authority for a conclusion; the evidence in each
        report must stand on its own.
      </p>

      <p>
        Competitive projects have also shaped the way I work under uncertainty.
        My teams placed first in QuantChallenge 2025 and received Jump Trading's
        Most Creative Algorithm award at Imperial Algothon 2026. The value of
        those exercises was not only the result, but the need to convert a view
        into testable rules, manage execution constraints and update quickly as
        new information arrived.
      </p>

      <h2>Evaluating a Thesis</h2>

      <p>
        Investment research should remain open to revision. An update should
        explain what changed, which assumptions survived, which weakened and
        whether the original risk framework remains appropriate.
      </p>

      <p>
        Failed theses will remain available. Removing unsuccessful ideas would
        create a cleaner record, but a less credible and less useful one. The
        important questions are whether the original process was reasonable,
        whether the risks were identified honestly and which assumption
        ultimately proved incorrect.
      </p>

      <p>
        Correct conclusions can also result from weak reasoning or favourable
        luck. Outcomes are therefore evaluated alongside the quality of the
        process rather than treated as a substitute for it.
      </p>

      <h2>How I Think About Uncertainty</h2>

      <p>
        One principle that has influenced my approach comes from the Bhagavad
        Gita: focus on the quality of your actions rather than becoming attached
        to their outcomes.
      </p>

      <p>
        In markets, that does not mean that outcomes are unimportant. It means
        separating the quality of a decision from the noise and randomness that
        may shape its immediate result. A carefully researched decision can
        lose money, while a poorly reasoned decision can occasionally be
        rewarded.
      </p>

      <p>
        The factors within my control are the depth of the research, the
        clarity of the assumptions, the treatment of risk and the willingness
        to change a view when better evidence emerges. A thesis should be held
        with enough conviction to act on it, but not with so much attachment
        that contradictory evidence is ignored.
      </p>

      <p>
        Criticism is therefore useful. A competing interpretation may reveal a
        missing variable, an underestimated risk or a weakness in the causal
        reasoning. Research improves when it is explicit enough to be
        challenged.
      </p>

      </ul>

      <p>

      </p>
