          <h2>Why I Built It</h2>

          <p>
            SM Research is a public record of how I analyse markets. Writing a view
            down imposes a higher standard than holding an informal opinion:
            assumptions become visible, evidence can be challenged and the
            conditions that would invalidate the conclusion must be stated in
            advance.
          </p>

          <p>
            The objective is not to present a catalogue of successful calls. It is
            to show how I frame questions, test evidence, size uncertainty and
            revise conclusions when the facts change. SM Research is the work of an
            individual analyst, not an investment firm or advisory service.
          </p>

          <h2>Why Commodities</h2>

          <p>
            My interest in markets began with risk transfer in the real economy.
            My mother's side of the family comes from a farming background, so the
            effects of weather, harvest conditions and commodity prices never felt
            abstract. Learning how futures can protect the value of a harvest,
            manage an airline's fuel exposure or secure an industrial input changed
            how I understood finance.
          </p>

          <p>
            Many of the earliest reports here originated while I served as
            Commodities Sector Head at Queen's Tower Capital, Imperial College
            London's student-managed investment fund. I led five analysts,
            coordinated sector coverage and presented recommendations through the
            fund's investment process.
          </p>

          <h2>How I Work</h2>

          <p>
            Each report begins with a defined investment question rather than a
            predetermined conclusion. The analysis considers physical supply and
            demand, market structure, macroeconomic conditions, valuation,
            positioning, catalysts and principal risks. Where the evidence permits,
            I specify a horizon, reference level, implementation framework and
            invalidation conditions.
          </p>

          <p>
            I prefer primary data and institutional sources, and try to keep facts,
            assumptions and interpretation visibly separate. Models are used where
            they make assumptions clearer or scenarios more testable. They are not
            used to add complexity for its own sake.
          </p>

          <p>
            Material errors are corrected rather than defended. The commodity-regime
            project, for example, preserves the original workflow while withdrawing
            conclusions that did not survive a data and specification audit.
          </p>

          <h2>Experience Informing the Work</h2>

          <p>
            I study Economics, Finance & Data Science at Imperial College London.
            Programmes at Barings, BNP Paribas and Millennium have exposed me to
            high-yield credit, cross-asset markets, market making and systematic
            research. Competitive projects have also tested my ability to research,
            build and communicate under time pressure, including a first-place team
            finish in QuantChallenge 2025.
          </p>

          <h2>Accountability</h2>

          <p>
            Failed theses remain published. Removing them would create a cleaner
            record, but a less credible one. The decision log reviews each view
            against its stated horizon and implementation without presenting the
            classifications as audited portfolio returns.
          </p>

          <p>
            The standard I am trying to develop is conviction without attachment:
            enough conviction to act on a well-supported view, but enough
            flexibility to change it when better evidence arrives. Both the process
            and the outcome matter.
          </p>
