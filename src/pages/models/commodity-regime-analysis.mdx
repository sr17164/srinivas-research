      <h2>Research Question</h2>

      <p>
        Does the relationship between commodity returns and the returns of
        commodity-producer equities change when inflation is already elevated?
        The project studies Brent crude against US energy equities, and gold
        and copper against US metals-and-mining equities.
      </p>

      <h2>Why the Original Model Was Revised</h2>

      <p>
        The initial workflow forward-filled missing monthly futures prices
        before calculating returns. That produced 14 observations in which
        Brent, gold and copper returns were all exactly zero while the producer
        equity ETFs moved. Treating those rows as genuine observations would
        mechanically weaken the estimated commodity-equity relationships.
      </p>

      <p>
        The revised project excludes those artificial rows, retains a full
        data-quality log and replaces separate regime regressions with a single
        interaction specification. The original script and report remain in
        the downloadable archive so that the change in methodology is visible.
      </p>

      <h2>Revised Specification</h2>

      <ul>
        <li>89 usable monthly observations from May 2017 to December 2025.</li>
        <li>
          High inflation is defined using the previous month's headline CPI
          rate, with 3% as the primary threshold.
        </li>
        <li>
          Energy and mining ETF returns are regressed on Brent, gold and copper
          returns, an inflation-regime indicator, three interaction terms and
          the monthly change in the US 10-year Treasury yield.
        </li>
        <li>
          Statistical inference uses Newey-West heteroskedasticity and
          autocorrelation-consistent standard errors with three lags.
        </li>
        <li>
          Robustness tests repeat the model at 2.68%, 3%, 3.5% and 4% CPI
          thresholds.
        </li>
      </ul>

      <p>
        The narrow defensible result is that Brent returns had a materially
        stronger relationship with energy-equity returns when inflation was
        already elevated. The corrected model does not support a statistically
        reliable inflation-regime shift for gold. Copper remains positively
        associated with mining equities, but its regime difference is not
        precise at the primary threshold.
      </p>

      <h2>Robustness and Interpretation</h2>

      <p>
        The Brent interaction remains positive and statistically significant
        across all four tested inflation thresholds. Copper's interaction
        becomes significant only at higher thresholds, so that result is
        sensitive to the chosen regime boundary. The models identify
        conditional associations, not causal effects.
      </p>

      <h2>Limitations</h2>

      <ul>
        <li>
          The retained market dataset contains prepared monthly returns rather
          than the original adjusted-close price series.
        </li>
        <li>
          XLE and XME are imperfect producer-equity proxies and contain
          company-specific and index-composition effects.
        </li>
        <li>
          The sample is modest, and isolated significant results should be
          interpreted cautiously in a multiple-testing setting.
        </li>
        <li>
          Inflation thresholds are modelling choices rather than natural
          economic boundaries.
        </li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Files and Reproducibility</h2>

      <p>
        The public package contains the audited dataset, exclusion log, Python
        analysis, complete regression output, threshold tests, figures,
        methodology notes and an Excel review model. The Excel workbook
        displays the Python-generated HAC estimates and exposes the data-quality
        and specification choices in a reviewable format.
      </p>
